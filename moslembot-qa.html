Q: What are the six pillars of Iman?

A: Based on authentic Islamic sources from the Qur'an and Sunnah, the Six Pillars of Iman (Faith) are as follows:

  1. Belief in Allah (Iman billah):
    To believe in Allah’s Oneness (Tawhid), His Lordship, His Names and Attributes, and that He alone deserves worship.
    “So know that there is no god except Allah…” (Surah Muhammad 47:19)
  2. Belief in His Angels (Iman bil-Mala'ikah):
    “The Messenger believes in what was revealed to him from his Lord, and so do the believers...” (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:285)
  3. Belief in His Revealed Books (Iman bil-Kutub):
    “…and We revealed the Torah, containing guidance and light...” (Surah Al-Ma’idah 5:44)
  4. Belief in His Messengers (Iman bil-Rusul):
    “And We certainly sent into every nation a messenger…” (Surah An-Nahl 16:36)
  5. Belief in the Last Day (Iman bil-Yawm al-Akhir):
    “...and of the Hereafter they are certain [in faith].” (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:4)
  6. Belief in Divine Decree (Qadar):
    “...and to believe in the Divine Decree—its good and bad.” (Sahih Muslim 8a)

Q: How does the Qur'an describe embryonic development stages?

A: Based solely on the Qur'an and authentic Sunnah, the embryonic development stages are described as follows:

  1. Creation from Dust/Clay:
    “And Allah created you from dust...” (Surah Fatir 35:11)
    “...then from a sperm-drop...” (Surah Al-Hajj 22:5)
    This affirms man's ultimate origin from the earth (Adam), followed by the biological process beginning with the nutfah (sperm-drop).
  2. The Stages within the Womb:
    Referencing Hadith of Ibn Mas'ud (Sahih Bukhari 7544, Jami' at-Tirmidhi 2284):
    “Indeed the creation of each one of you is brought together in his mother's womb for forty days as a nutfah... then 'alaqah... then mudghah...”
    • Stage 1 (40 days): Nutfah – mingling of fluids
    • Stage 2 (40 days): 'Alaqah – clinging clot (like a leech)
    • Stage 3 (40 days): Mudghah – chewed-like lump with somites
  3. Ensoulment and Further Development:
    “Then the angel is sent... and breathes the soul into him...”
    This occurs after 120 days. Then Allah commands the angel to write provisions, lifespan, deeds, and final outcome. It aligns with:
    “...then [Allah] brings you forth as infants...” (Surah Al-Hajj 22:5)
  4. Allah’s Absolute Knowledge:
    Even before formation, Allah knows what’s in the womb. Gender, outcome, or soul are matters of the unseen (ghayb). Only after tasweer (shaping) does the angel receive divine instructions.

Summary:
Qur’anic verses (especially Surah Al-Hajj 22:5) and Hadith of Ibn Mas'ud describe a clear sequence: Nutfah → 'Alaqah → Mudghah over 120 days, followed by ensoulment. While we can describe physical stages, full knowledge belongs to Allah alone.

Q: Why is Riba prohibited in economic transactions?

A: Based solely on Islamic sources (Qur'an, Sunnah, and scholarly consensus), the prohibition of Riba (usury/interest) is absolute. Here’s why:

  1. Divine Command & Clear Prohibition:
    “But Allah has permitted trading and forbidden interest...” (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:275)
    “...taking interest despite its prohibition...” (Surah An-Nisa' 4:161)
    Riba is not just discouraged; it is outright haram by explicit divine decree.
  2. Injustice and Illicit Consumption of Wealth:
    “...and consuming people’s wealth unjustly...” (Surah An-Nisa' 4:161)
    Riba exploits the need of borrowers—transferring wealth to lenders without equivalent value or shared risk.
  3. Violation of Mutual Consent Based on Justice:
    Islam requires mutual agreement within lawful bounds:
    “Do not devour one another’s wealth illegally, but rather trade by mutual consent.” (Surah An-Nisa’ 4:29)
    Even if both parties agree to a Riba-based deal, that consent is invalid if it violates Shari'ah.
    Scholars clarify: contentment only makes wealth lawful when the means are also lawful.
  4. Economic Harm and Imbalance:
    “There is no one who deals in usury a great deal but he will end up with little.” (Sunan Ibn Majah 2365)
    Riba provides unearned gain to the lender and long-term loss to the borrower, fostering economic instability.
  5. Distinction from Lawful Trade:
    “But Allah has permitted trading and forbidden interest...” (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:275)
    Trade is productive, reciprocal, and risk-based. Riba guarantees return without contribution or effort.
  6. Severe Spiritual Consequences:
    Those who persist in Riba are warned of divine punishment:
    “Those who consume interest will stand [on Judgment Day] like those driven to madness by Satan’s touch...” (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:275)
    Riba is not a minor sin—it incurs Allah’s anger and ultimate accountability in the Hereafter.

In Summary:

  • Riba is explicitly prohibited in the Qur'an and Sunnah.
  • It involves unjust gain and exploitation of need.
  • Consent does not justify what Allah has made haram.
  • It destabilizes society and magnifies inequality.
  • It contrasts lawful trade which shares risk and effort.
  • It brings serious spiritual danger and loss in the Hereafter.

Q: What etiquette governs eating and drinking in Islam?

A: Islamic teachings offer detailed and merciful guidance on the proper manners (adab) of eating and drinking. These etiquettes reflect cleanliness, moderation, gratitude, and mindfulness of Allah (SWT).

General Etiquette:

  • Begin with Allah's Name: Say “Bismillah” before eating. If forgotten, say “Bismillahi awwalahu wa akhirahu.” (Sahih Muslim)
  • End with Praise: Say “Alhamdulillah” after eating. (Sunan Abi Dawud, Tirmidhi)
  • Use the Right Hand: Eat and drink with the right hand. (Sahih Muslim)
  • Eat What is Before You: Do not reach across others; eat from the side nearest to you. (Sahih Bukhari, Muslim)
  • Moderation: Avoid overeating.
    “The son of Adam does not fill any vessel worse than his stomach...” (Sunan Ibn Majah, Tirmidhi – Hasan)
  • No Wastefulness:
    “...eat and drink but be not excessive. Indeed, He likes not those who commit excess.” (Qur’an 7:31)
  • Share Food: Sharing is encouraged, even when food is little. (Sahih Bukhari)
  • Respect Food: Do not criticize food. If disliked, leave it silently. (Sahih Bukhari, Muslim)
  • Cleanliness: Wash hands before and after eating. (Sunan Abi Dawud)
  • Sitting Position: Eat while sitting (on the ground or low table). Avoid reclining. (Sahih Bukhari)
  • After Eating: Lick fingers before washing to respect food remnants. (Sahih Muslim)

Specific Drinking Etiquette:

  • Drink in Sips: Drink in 2–3 sips, not all at once. (Sahih Bukhari, Muslim)
  • Do Not Blow into the Drink: (Sahih Bukhari)
  • Do Not Drink from a Jug Directly: Pour into a cup or glass. (Sahih Bukhari)
  • Use the Right Hand: While drinking. (Sahih Bukhari)

Prohibitions:

  • Gold and Silver Vessels: Strictly forbidden to eat or drink from them. (Sahih Muslim)
  • Intoxicants: All forms are absolutely haram.
    “O you who have believed, indeed, intoxicants... are but defilement...” (Qur’an 5:90)

In Summary: Eating and drinking in Islam are not mere habits but acts of worship and discipline. They are governed by gratitude, cleanliness, respect for others and food, moderation, and mindfulness of Allah’s blessings.
